Student Affairs Philosophy
In Fall of 2022, I had the enlightening opportunity to participate in an exercise to reflect on my personal higher education/student affairs philosophy. Having been in the master’s in Higher Education program for over a year now as well as transitioning from a part-time graduate assistant to full-time staff, I would like to take this time to update my personal philosophy as it may have evolved with my experiences.
As my career has developed over the last year, thanks in part to work I have completed in courses such as this one, my philosophy on higher education has grown with it. The most marked change in my philosophy is the increased emphasis on the wellbeing of staff and faculty as cornerstone of the successful administration of an institution of higher education. Experiences that I have had over the last year as a full-time student affairs professional combined with research I have done in the course of my master’s program have brought to light just how important it is for university staff and faculty to feel supported by their administration in achieving job satisfaction and the professional goals they set out for themselves.
First, part of my philosophy of higher education is the large amount of responsibility I place on upper-level administrators to support and implement intuitional level policies that aid in job satisfaction for all staff. Sell (2023) emphasizes the importance of the implementation of organizational policies to increasing job satisfaction among student affairs professionals. My philosophy of student affairs aligns with this way of thinking and will work to advocate for such policies in my student affairs practice. Practically these policies can look like flexible work schedules and environments, ample opportunities for professional development, and mechanisms to provide feedback to administrators.
Second, through my experience this past year as a student affairs professional and student of higher education, I have truly come to value transparent communication as a means of supporting student affairs staff. Solomon & Tierney (1977) discuss the importance of transparency in how the reward structure matches the communicated institutional values. It is essential in my view for administrators to accurately communicate the reward structure of their division or institution and how that aligns with the values of the institution itself. For instance, an institution should ensure that they are prioritizing internal promotion opportunities if they communicate that they value developing their staff and increasing staff retention.
Finally, as another component of my updated student affairs philosophy, I’d like to talk a bit about how student affairs professionals perceive their fit for the role and the institution they work in. As I previously talked about transparent communication, in my view this practice should start before an applicant even submits their resume and cover letter. Organizations should make their expectations and values clear on any public materials or application portal so that any potential candidates are fully aware of how they might fit into the organizational structure and culture. Likewise, candidates should be extremely diligent in their research of universities they are interested in being a part of. If job satisfaction is an ultimate goal of the employer-employee relationship, then transparent communication when finding the right fit is essential (Issah, 2021).
Ultimately, my student affairs philosophy has changed in that I value even more the role that staff and faculty play in ensuring a healthy and successful campus environment for students. Students are always the focus of everything we student affairs professionals do, but we have to set a good foundation for our staff and faculty to work from. We cannot support our students and meet one hundred percent of their needs if staff to have the backing of administrative policies that promote well-being and transparent communication, particularly communication which allows for both employees and employers to find the best fit with each other.
